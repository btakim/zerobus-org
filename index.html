What is ZeroBus?

An independent mobility research initiative that maps viable electric‑coach corridors in Europe and aims to publish practical guidance on the transformation of electric coach industry in the form of white paper for bus operators, bus terminal operators, OEMs, charging providers, and public bodies.

Research only — we don’t operate busses or sell tickets. Our focus is on developing the models necessary to find out answers and figure out what the industry will need during this transformation phase.

Why does ZeroBus exist?

  • Climate‑Aware Demand: Travelers will choose low‑carbon modes when reliability, comfort, and price align.
  • EU Goals: Green Deal / Fit‑for‑55 push deep transport decarbonization; operators need data‑backed playbooks.
  • Tech Wave: New e‑coaches from European OEMs arrive through 2026; planning now enables early pilots.
  • Transform: We want to catalyze the change in intercity bus industry with zero-emission vehicles.

Why now?

Technology

Next‑generation electric coaches with ranges of more than 500 kilometres are rolling out from European vehicle manufacturers (for example, Daimler and MAN) through 2026.

Policy & Incentives

European Union and national frameworks support zero‑emission fleets and pilots, aligning public goals with operator economics.

Amenity Leap

Reliable connectivity, USB-C charging ports at each seat, and Qi2 wireless charging enable a meaningfully better onboard experience.

Rise of Artificial Intelligence

Rapid advances in artificial intelligence and supporting tools are lowering the cost of building smarter operations and customer experiences — from demand forecasting and dynamic pricing to reliable, multilingual chatbots and self‑service applications. Smaller operators can now deploy capabilities that were once limited to companies with very large budgets.

Changing Demand

Climate‑aware travel preferences, increasingly frequent extreme‑weather events, and a younger generation that values sustainability are shifting expectations toward zero‑emission options — provided reliability, comfort, and price are competitive.

Unsatisfactory Train Services

In several markets, rail reliability and customer satisfaction lag expectations — for example, recurring delays and overcrowding reported by Deutsche Bahn passengers. This creates space for high‑quality electric coach services to capture demand on short‑to‑medium corridors.

Problem

Limited Sustainable Choices

Rail depends on long‑cycle infrastructure upgrades with uneven national progress; zero-emission commercial flights are not yet in sight, current fleets are fast but carbon‑intensive on <500 km city pairs; diesel coaches remain cheap but polluting and uncomfortable.

Outdated Customer Experience

Across travel modes, passengers face unreliable wireless internet, limited power, and dated interiors — misaligned with modern expectations for connected work and leisure. Furthermore, even basic necessities such as warm water and snacks are often not available during the trip.

Research Questions

Operations

Which 200 to 500 kilometre corridors can meet service frequency and reliability with current or near‑term vehicle capabilities?

Charging & Grid

What mix of depot and en‑route charging works by corridor given grid capacity and tariffs?

Economics

How does total cost of ownership compare with diesel under realistic utilisation, financing, incentives, and maintenance?

Passenger Experience

Which amenities (wireless internet, device charging, comfortable seating, quiet cabins, and basic necessities such as water and snacks) encourage people to switch travel modes and pay for better service?

Scaling Path

What pilot design (fleet size, duration, and key performance indicators) reduces risk and supports expansion across multiple city pairs?

Data & Telemetry

Which data (vehicle, charging, environmental) is necessary to monitor reliability and optimize operations, and how should it be shared across stakeholders?

Method

Data Modeling

Our energy models account for real‑world details such as hills, heating and cooling, time spent at stops, and the impact of different seasons.

Interviews

Structured conversations with operators, intercity passengers, vehicle manufacturers, charging providers, electricity distribution system operators, and public authorities.

Comparative Analysis

Time, price, and carbon dioxide (CO2) trade‑offs between rail, air, and coach on target corridors.

Participate

Bus Operators

Share target corridors, schedules, fleet constraints, turnarounds, and reliability pain points to shape realistic duty cycles and pilot designs.

Vehicle Manufacturers

Provide vehicle specifications, charging envelopes, delivery timelines, and field performance data to validate range, uptime, and total cost of ownership assumptions.

Charging & Distribution System Operators

Co‑develop site plans, capacity roadmaps, and tariff structures; outline grid constraints and permit timelines along target corridors.

Passengers

Tell us what matters on board (comfort, quietness, connectivity, power, refreshments) and which trade‑offs would make you switch modes.

Bus Terminal Operators

Outline gate capacity, passenger flows, amenities, charging feasibility, and operations constraints to inform terminal‑ready rollouts.

Advocates

Help assess equity, accessibility, and climate impact; share insights on policy levers and community priorities along proposed routes.
